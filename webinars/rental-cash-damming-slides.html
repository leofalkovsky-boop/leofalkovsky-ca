The Problem

Most Landlords Are Structured the Wrong Way

1
You have a personal home mortgage — interest is NOT tax-deductible
2
You have a rental property — rental expenses ARE deductible
3
You're paying personal mortgage interest with after-tax dollars — the most expensive kind
Example: $450,000 personal mortgage at 5.5% = $24,750/year in interest. Every dollar of that costs you $1.67 if you're in a 40% tax bracket.
The Legal Foundation

The CRA Purpose Test

"Interest on money borrowed to earn income from a business or property is deductible."
— CRA IT-533 / Income Tax Act s. 20(1)(c)
  • Personal mortgage = borrowed to own your home = NOT deductible
  • HELOC used to fund rental expenses = borrowed to earn rental income = DEDUCTIBLE
  • Cash Damming is built entirely on this distinction — it's well-established and CRA-recognized
The Strategy

How Cash Damming Works

1
Deposit all rental income onto your personal mortgage
2
Draw from a HELOC to pay all rental property expenses
3
HELOC interest = fully deductible (used for income-producing purpose)
4
Repeat monthly — personal mortgage shrinks, deductible debt grows

It Gets Better Every Year

  • Year 3: ~$64,800 of deductible HELOC → ~$25,920 refund
  • Year 5: ~$108,000 of deductible HELOC → ~$43,200 refund

  • The strategy snowballs: more deductible debt = bigger refund = more to redeploy
Key insight: You're not paying more each month. You're restructuring which account dollars flow through. The tax refund is the return on zero extra spending.

Q&A

Questions I Hear Every Time

Is this legal?

Yes — it's based on CRA IT-533 and the purpose test. The paper trail is what matters. Done right, it's bulletproof.

Does my rental need to be profitable?

No. Even cash-flow neutral rentals benefit — the tax refund often creates positive cash flow.

What if I only have one property?

Cash Damming needs a rental. If you only have a personal home, ask me about the Smith Manoeuvre — same principle, different execution.
