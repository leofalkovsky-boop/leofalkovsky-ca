Cash Damming

Rental Cash Damming in 2026:
Why Debt Structure Matters

Dec 2, 2025  ·  5 min read  ·  By Leo Falkovsky

When rental property yields are thin and carrying costs are elevated, the difference between a profitable portfolio and a struggling one is often not the property itself — it's how the debt is structured. Cash damming is one of the most powerful and underused tools available to Canadian real estate investors, and in 2026, it deserves serious attention.

What Is Cash Damming?

Cash damming is a CRA-recognized debt restructuring strategy that converts non-deductible personal mortgage interest into tax-deductible investment interest. It works by redirecting rental income to pay down your personal (non-deductible) mortgage, while using a separate line of credit to pay rental property expenses — making the LOC interest tax-deductible.

The result: over time, your non-deductible personal debt shrinks while your deductible investment debt grows. You're not taking on more debt — you're restructuring existing debt so that a larger portion of your interest expense qualifies for a tax deduction.

Why 2026 Makes This More Relevant

With 5-year fixed rates in the 4.5–5.5% range and variable rates still above historical norms, the interest cost on rental properties is materially higher than it was in 2020 or 2021. Cash flow is tighter. Every dollar of deductible interest is worth more.

Consider: if your marginal tax rate is 43% (a common bracket for dual-income Ontario households), making $10,000 of interest tax-deductible saves you $4,300 per year. Over a 5-year term, that's $21,500 — real money that compounds into your next investment.

A Step-by-Step Example

Scenario: $450,000 rental property, $3,200/mo rent

  1. Step 1 — Collect rent to a dedicated account. All $3,200 monthly rental income goes into a separate account earmarked for personal mortgage payments only.
  2. Step 2 — Pay personal mortgage with rental income. Apply the $3,200 directly against your personal home mortgage, reducing the non-deductible balance faster.
  3. Step 3 — Use LOC for all rental expenses. Your rental property expenses — mortgage payment ($2,400), property tax ($350), insurance ($80), repairs — are paid entirely from your LOC, not from rental income.
  4. Step 4 — LOC interest becomes deductible. Because the LOC funds are used to earn income (rental income), the interest on the LOC is deductible under CRA's income-earning purpose test.
  5. Step 5 — Repeat monthly. Each cycle, your personal mortgage balance drops and your deductible LOC balance grows. The personal mortgage extinguishes faster; the tax deduction grows.

The CRA Tracing Rules

Cash damming only works if the paper trail is clean. CRA's "direct tracing" rule requires that borrowed funds (from the LOC) be demonstrably used to earn income. This means:

  • Keep a dedicated LOC — do not co-mingle personal and rental expenses in the same account
  • Document every rental expense paid from the LOC with receipts and statements
  • Never use the LOC for personal purchases — this contaminates the deductibility of the interest
  • Keep a running log that your accountant can reference at tax time

Done correctly, cash damming has been upheld consistently by CRA. Done sloppily, it creates audit risk and potential reassessment. The discipline is in the bookkeeping, not the math.

What You Need to Set It Up

Cash damming requires a readvanceable mortgage or a separate HELOC/LOC on either your personal or rental property. Ideally, you want a product like Manulife One (where the mortgage and LOC are integrated and balances shift automatically) or a standard setup where you maintain separate accounts with clear transaction trails.

Your accountant should be involved in the setup — not because it's legally complex, but because proper documentation from day one is what makes the deduction defensible. I work directly with clients and their accountants to set up the product structure correctly before the first dollar moves.

Common Mistakes

  • Mixing LOC uses. If the LOC is used for even one personal expense, that portion of interest loses deductibility — and the tracing becomes disputed for the whole account.
  • No dedicated rental income account. Rental income that goes into a general chequing account and gets spent on anything else breaks the flow. Use a dedicated account.
  • Waiting until you have a large rental portfolio. Cash damming works on a single rental property. Starting early maximizes the cumulative tax benefit over the life of your personal mortgage.

In an environment where margins are tight and carrying costs are elevated, the investors pulling ahead are the ones optimizing the structure of what they already own — not just chasing new acquisitions. Cash damming is one of the most direct ways to do that.
